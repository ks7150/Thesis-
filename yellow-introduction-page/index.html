In this painting, the yellow background dominates the scene, acting as a backdrop and a spiritual field of energy and divinity. The central figure, most likely Vishnu or Krishna, stands in a landscape teeming with flora and fauna, surrounded by divine and semi-divine figures like Brahma, Shiva, Ganesha, and devotees. Here, yellow symbolizes cosmic radiance, enlightenment, and divine presence. In traditional Indian symbology, yellow is often associated with purity, auspiciousness, and celestial realms. It creates an ethereal setting that elevates the divinity, reinforcing the mythological theme of celestial assembly and devotion.
This painting, most likely belonging to the Mughal school, uses yellow much more decoratively and contextually. Here, yellow appears in fabrics, architectural details, and ceremonial elements, enhancing the grandeur and richness of the royal court scene. The theme revolves around imperial authority, hierarchy, and pageantry. The use of yellow is integrated into a more detailed and ornate visual grammar—layered textures, embroidered garments, gilded furniture—underscoring wealth, opulence, and refinement. Unlike the spiritual transcendence in most paintings, yellow here becomes a marker of luxury, status, and power, harmonizing with red and green to form a complex visual vocabulary of prestige and control.
Krishna, painted in a deep indigo blue with a vivid yellow lower garment, becomes the clear focal point, standing at the center of the composition and visually anchoring the chaotic crowd and natural world around him. The yellow in this context radiates heroism, divine courage, and protective power. Thematically, the painting revolves around divine intervention, devotion, and cosmic protection, but Mughal artistry reinterprets this with an emphasis on naturalism and texture. Depicting the famous episode of Krishna lifting Mount Govardhan to protect the villagers of Vrindavan from Indra's wrath. What is most compelling in this scene is the way color—especially yellow—is used to bridge mythological storytelling with the Mughal aesthetic of realism and detail.
